# Models for seeing the situation clearly (ladder rungs 1-2)

Original one-line glosses, each with a trigger for when to reach for it. Pull a
model only when it changes the decision.

- **Frame of reference.** What you observe depends on where you stand. Before judging, ask whose vantage point you are using and how it distorts.
- **Scale.** Behavior changes with size; few things stay linear as they grow. Ask whether what holds at today's size still holds at the size you are heading for.
- **Equilibrium.** The resting state a system returns to once forces balance. Ask whether you are fighting the equilibrium — which gets erased — or moving it, which is the only change that lasts.
- **Regression to the mean.** Extremes tend to be followed by less extreme outcomes. Do not mistake a return to average for an effect you caused.
- **Base rates and updating.** Start from how often this kind of thing happens in general, then move your estimate in proportion to the strength of new evidence.
- **Niches.** Survival comes from fitting an environment, not raw strength. Read which niche actually rewards what you have.
- **Scarcity.** Finite supply against want sets value and forces choice. Ask what is genuinely scarce here, and what only looks scarce.
- **Supply and demand.** Price and quantity emerge where what is offered meets what is wanted. Ask which side you can actually shift, and where the resting point moves when you do.
- **Reversible or irreversible.** One-way doors deserve deliberation; two-way doors you can walk back. Match the care you spend to whether the choice can be undone.
- **Chesterton's fence.** Do not remove or change something until you understand why it was put there. The original reason may still hold.
- **Survivorship bias.** You see the winners, not the graveyard. Ask where the failures went before you generalize from the survivors — reasoning from winners alone overstates the odds.
