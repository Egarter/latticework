# Models for seeing the situation clearly (ladder rungs 1-2)

Original one-line glosses. Pull a model only when it changes the decision.

- **Frame of reference.** What you observe depends on where you stand. Before judging, ask whose vantage point you are using and how it distorts.
- **Scale.** Behavior changes with size. What holds at one scale can break at another; few things stay linear as they grow.
- **Equilibrium.** The resting state a system returns to once forces balance. A push that ignores the equilibrium gets erased; lasting change moves the equilibrium itself.
- **Regression to the mean.** Extremes tend to be followed by less extreme outcomes. Do not mistake a return to average for an effect you caused.
- **Base rates and updating.** Start from how often this kind of thing happens in general, then move your estimate in proportion to the strength of new evidence.
- **Niches.** Survival comes from fitting an environment, not raw strength. Read which niche actually rewards what you have.
- **Scarcity.** Finite supply against want sets value and forces choice. Ask what is genuinely scarce here, and what only looks scarce.
- **Supply and demand.** Price and quantity emerge where what is offered meets what is wanted. Shift either side and the resting point moves.
- **Reversible or irreversible.** One-way doors deserve deliberation; two-way doors you can walk back, so decide fast and adjust. Match the care you spend to whether the choice can be undone.
- **Chesterton's fence.** Do not remove or change something until you understand why it was put there. The original reason may still hold.
- **Survivorship bias.** You see the winners, not the graveyard. Reasoning from survivors alone overstates the odds and hides what killed everyone else.
