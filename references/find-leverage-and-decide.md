# Models for finding leverage and choosing (ladder rungs 4-5)

Original one-line glosses, each with a trigger for when to reach for it. Pull a
model only when it changes the decision.

- **Bottlenecks.** One constraint sets the pace of the whole. Find that constraint before optimizing anything else — effort spent elsewhere is mostly wasted.
- **Leverage.** A small force, well placed, moves a large load. Look for the point where modest effort has outsized effect.
- **Activation energy.** The upfront cost to start something that then runs on its own. Ask whether lowering it is the real game here — often it is.
- **Catalysts.** Something that speeds a change without being consumed. Find the person, tool, or condition that accelerates everything near it.
- **Margin of safety.** Build in slack so being wrong is survivable. Size the margin to the cost of failure.
- **Friction.** The drag that drops real execution below the frictionless plan. Budget for it rather than pretending it away.
- **Velocity over speed.** Direction times speed. Check the direction before you celebrate the pace — fast motion the wrong way is not progress.
- **The zero factor (multiplicative term).** One fatal term collapses the whole product, however strong the rest. Find the factor that can wipe you out.
- **Comparative advantage.** Specialize where you give up the least to act, then trade. Decide on relative, not absolute, strength.
- **Opportunity cost.** The real cost of a choice is the best thing you gave up for it, not the cash you spent. Name the best alternative you are forgoing.
- **Trade-offs.** You cannot maximize everything at once. Name what you will give up, or it gets decided for you.
- **Incentives.** People move toward reward and away from cost. To predict behavior, read the incentives, not the stated intentions.
- **Alloying (combination effects).** Combining elements yields properties none had alone. When no single option suffices, ask what mixture produces a property none of the inputs have.
- **Dependency.** What relies on what. Trace what this rests on and find the hidden single dependency — it is a hidden single point of failure.
- **Sunk cost.** What you have already spent is gone whatever you choose next. Decide from where you stand now, not from what you have poured in.
- **Expected value.** Weigh each outcome by its payoff times its probability. A small chance of a large result can beat a sure small one, and the reverse.
- **Decision vs outcome.** A choice can be sound and still lose, or reckless and still win. Judge the decision by what was knowable at the time, not by how it turned out.
- **Asymmetry and optionality.** Favor bets where the downside is capped and the upside stays open. Pay a little to keep good surprises possible and bad ones survivable.
